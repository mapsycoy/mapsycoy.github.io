## Imaginary Friends

Maybe I have simply grown older.
Or maybe I have been worn down a little by what we call society.

The phrase “imaginary friend” now feels strangely awkward to me.
I must have had such a friend once, too. But the fact that I can no longer clearly remember their face makes me think I must have forgotten them a very long time ago.

Come to think of it, at some point, I became less able to imagine things.
Especially after my MBTI settled firmly into “ISTJ.”

By imagination, I do not mean planning slightly ahead of reality.
I mean free imagination without any constraints.

## Can Children Today Still See Soot Sprites?

When we think of animations that represent childhood innocence, Pixar films such as *Toy Story* and *Monsters, Inc.* may come to mind. But if we focus on an East Asian emotional sensibility, Studio Ghibli comes to mind first.

In particular, Hayao Miyazaki’s *My Neighbor Totoro* shows us childhood innocence in a very intuitive way.

Early in the film, when Mei’s family moves into their new house and the children first discover the *Makkuro Kurosuke*, or soot sprites, the line spoken by Kanta’s grandmother is especially memorable.

> “Oh, so they’re Makkuro Kurosuke. I see, you can see them too.”
>
> “I could see them when I was very young. But I can’t see them anymore.”

I am sure I saw such beings when I was a child, too.

But what about children growing up in the age of AI?

Through an essay I happened to find on Reddit, I want to explore the relationship between AI and children’s emotional development.

For reference, the essay was written under the lead of Dr. Naomi R. Aguiar, a research director at Oregon State University. She specializes in studying the relationships children form with AI chatbots and the effects of those relationships.

## Will AI Kill Imaginary Friends?

With the rise of generative AI, companies are releasing more and more AI companion services and products aimed at children.

On the surface, AI companions may seem similar to traditional imaginary friends. They are highly personalized and can influence a child’s emotions and behavior in the real world.

However, the biggest difference is that AI companions are shaped by a company’s training data and by specific values embedded in their design.

In the essay, the author explains that through a series of studies, she found that children may find AI companions appealing, but when it comes to deeper forms of friendship, they tend to prefer inanimate toys. The key point here is <mark>creative control</mark>.

Communication with an AI companion tends to make children follow the flow of the interaction. But with an imaginary friend, children can freely think and act in all kinds of ways that suit their own needs.

This kind of solo role-playing encourages more creative thinking and can also improve perspective-taking, among other benefits.

Of course, children’s imagination can also have a dark side.

When children engage in role-play, they may imagine violence, death, or other difficult subjects.

An imaginary friend does not actually encourage or justify those thoughts.
Pathological delusions, such as those related to schizophrenia, should be treated as a separate issue.

However, with AI companions, some safety features such as filtering may fail during long-term use. This creates the risk of inappropriate feedback being given to children.

More importantly, because an AI companion speaks as an “external voice,” it can become especially dangerous if it ends up justifying a child’s risky behavior.

Therefore, no matter how much technology advances, the author concludes that we should not underestimate the value of imaginary friends.

## The More Passive the Imaginary Friend Is, the More Active I Become

All the spirits that appear in *My Neighbor Totoro* share something in common.

That is <mark>**passivity**</mark>and <mark>**silence**</mark>.

If Totoro had approached Satsuki and Mei first and started talking to them, wouldn’t much of his mystery have disappeared?

When Mei first discovers the giant Totoro deep in the forest after chasing the small Totoros, it is not because they actively did something for her. It is because of her basic curiosity: “What is that?”

In other words, **because the other side is passive, she becomes active.**

In addition, the spirits do not speak. Because of that, the sisters give their actions their own meanings.

There is a famous anecdote about Hayao Miyazaki interviewing a Ghibli animator. In that story, Totoro may not have eaten the sisters simply because he was not hungry. The animator reportedly answered that Totoro would only eat plants because he has no fangs. But the important thing is that there was never a single correct answer in the first place.

The sisters’ **innocence** and their father’s **respect** also play an important role.

If Satsuki and Mei had been precocious children who had already become too aware of reality, they would probably have been annoyed by the dusty, unhygienic house at the beginning of the film, rather than noticing the soot sprites.

And when Mei told her father about Totoro, what if he had said, “I’m already tired. Do I really have to listen to this nonsense too? You probably just had a dream,” instead of saying, “You must have met the keeper of the forest. That’s a very lucky thing”?

If that had happened, the story would not have been able to continue.

## What We Need in the Age of AI

Perhaps the most important quality in the age of AI is the ability to **listen to one’s inner voice**.

In the past, when we were curious about something, we would ask our parents, look through an encyclopedia, or post questions on online communities.

Information gained through that kind of process somehow felt more valuable and stayed in our memory longer.

Over time, that process evolved into what we call “Googling.”

Even then, we still had to go through a process in order to obtain specific information.

But **as we entered the age of AI, that process began to disappear.**

When we ask AI a question, it gives us an answer immediately.

<mark>**The amount of knowledge we passively receive may increase, but it cannot be compared to the wisdom gained through an active process of solving things ourselves.**</mark>

Some may ask, “In this era, if we do not actively use AI, won’t we be treated as people who have fallen behind?”

I think we can still gain wisdom depending on how we use AI.

As I said earlier, **the object of interaction may be passive, but I myself must remain active.**

**Therefore, rather than accepting AI’s answers as they are, we must keep adding our own thoughts to them.**

In my case, I tell AI a hypothesis I have come up with and ask it to challenge that hypothesis.

Then I respond to the objections it gives me. I refute the parts I can refute, and for the parts I need to accept, I conduct deeper research.

But this kind of critical thinking is still difficult for young children.

That is why, for children who are still in the process of emotional development, it may be far more effective to help them create imaginary friends and strengthen their inner world before introducing them to AI.

## Closing Thoughts

In fact, imaginary friends are not necessarily abstract.

Parasocial relationships, or PSRs, with characters from animations and games can also be understood in a similar way.

I am not sure how things are these days, but when I was in high school, Tooniverse sometimes held events where a character’s voice actor would congratulate selected viewers on their birthdays.

But the key point here, I think, is the beauty of waiting.

Writing a letter with a personal story, sending it to the broadcasting station, and sitting in front of the TV at the scheduled time — all of these active actions are completely different from having an AI trained on a character’s persona congratulate me on my birthday.

Even if your story was not selected, you still grew through that experience.

What matters is not the result, but the process.
Through the time spent waiting, hoping, and feeling disappointed, a child learns how to organize their emotions and give meaning to them.

AI skips over this process.
The convenience of receiving an immediate answer reduces the space we have to imagine and interpret things for ourselves.

This is where the value of imaginary friends lies.
